The Real History of Race Theory

Race isn't biology. It's a story we were told.

December 2025 · 18 min read

We Are One Species

Let's start with the science, because the science is settled.

Every human being on this planet — all eight billion of us — belongs to the same species: Homo sapiens. Not different races. Not subspecies. Not separate branches of humanity. One species. Unified, not divided.

This isn't opinion. This isn't politics. This is the conclusion of the Human Genome Project, completed in 2003, which sequenced the entire human genetic code. The verdict? The DNA of all human beings living today is 99.9% identical.

That 0.1% difference that does exist between individuals? It doesn't sort neatly into what we call "races." Geneticist Richard Lewontin demonstrated this back in 1972: only about 6% of human genetic variation falls between so-called racial groups. The remaining 94%? It exists within any population you care to name. A village in Norway. A city in Nigeria. A town in Tennessee.

"There's more genetic diversity within a group of chimps on a single hillside in Gombe than in the entire human species." — Race: The Power of an Illusion, PBS

Let that sink in. Chimpanzees separated by a river are more genetically different from each other than a person from Iceland and a person from Papua New Guinea. Oxford University researchers confirmed this in 2012: chimps from different populations — living in the same country, sometimes just across a river — are "substantially more different genetically than humans living on different continents."

We are, scientifically speaking, remarkably — almost suspiciously — similar to one another.

Why We're So Genetically Uniform

Two things create subspecies in the animal kingdom: isolation and time.

Humans have neither.

Modern humans — Homo sapiens sapiens — emerged in Africa roughly 300,000 years ago. We spent most of our evolution together, on the same continent. We only left Africa 50,000 to 100,000 years ago, spreading rapidly across the globe. In evolutionary terms, that's the blink of an eye.

And unlike other animals, we never stayed put. We forded rivers. We crossed mountains. We sailed oceans. We traded, migrated, conquered, and intermarried. Even the most remote island populations originally came from somewhere else — and maintained contact with neighboring groups.

As the Smithsonian's Human Origins Program puts it: "We all have roots extending back 300,000 years to the emergence of the first modern humans in Africa, and back more than 6 million years to the evolution of the earliest human species in Africa. This amazing story of adaptation and survival is written in the language of our genes, in every cell of our bodies."

There hasn't been enough time — and we've never been isolated enough — to evolve into different subspecies. The genetic variation simply isn't there.

Skin Deep: The Illusion of Difference

But what about the obvious differences? The skin colors, the hair textures, the facial features?

They're real. And they're meaningless.

Human variation is what scientists call "non-concordant." That means traits are inherited independently, not bundled together in neat packages. You can't predict someone's blood type from their skin color. You can't determine their intelligence from the shape of their nose. You can't tell anything about someone's character, abilities, or worth from the adaptations their ancestors developed to survive in different climates.

Skin color, for instance, follows what scientists call a "clinal" distribution — a continuous gradient with no clear dividing lines. Human skin tones shift gradually from the equator toward the poles, tracking UV radiation exposure. It's an adaptation, like the length of a fox's ears or the thickness of a bear's fur. Nothing more.

Dr. Nina Jablonski, an anthropologist at Penn State who has spent decades studying human skin pigmentation, puts it simply: "There are no genes unique to any particular race."

The visual differences we've been trained to obsess over tell us nothing about what's beneath the skin. As one Stanford geneticist explained: "Our physical differences are actually really superficial in terms of genetics. We humans share 99.9% of our DNA with each other! And the 0.1% of DNA that is different between humans doesn't align neatly with race... This makes us far too similar to one another to be considered different subspecies."

So Where Did "Race" Come From?

If race isn't biological, where did the concept come from?

We made it up.

The word "race" — applied to groups of humans — didn't exist until the late 16th century. The first recorded use of "race" to categorize human populations appeared in 1684, in a French travel writer's observations. The word "white" as a legal and social category first appeared in the American colonies in 1691.

And here's the part they don't want you to understand: race was invented AFTER slavery began, to JUSTIFY it.

Before European colonization, no group of humans considered themselves a unified "race." The people of England didn't think of themselves as the same category as the people of Spain, who didn't think of themselves as the same as the people of Russia. Similarly, the diverse peoples of Africa — with thousands of distinct languages, cultures, and ethnic identities — didn't think of themselves as a single group.

In fact, early European accounts of African peoples were often positive. Fifteenth and sixteenth-century Europeans described Africans as skilled farmers, sophisticated craftsmen, and capable traders. The initial image was not one of inferiority.

So what changed?

Money. Power. And the need to sleep at night.

A Timeline of Invention

1452-1493: The Doctrine of Discovery. Pope Nicholas V issues Dum Diversas, authorizing Portugal to enslave non-Christians. Pope Alexander VI follows with papal bulls dividing the "New World" between Spain and Portugal. The religious justification comes first: these aren't Christians, so they can be enslaved.

1676: Bacon's Rebellion in Virginia. Poor white indentured servants and enslaved Black workers unite against the colonial elite. They burn Jamestown to the ground. The planter class is terrified. Their solution? Drive a wedge between the groups. Give poor whites just enough privilege to identify with their masters rather than their fellow laborers.

1691: Virginia becomes the first colony to use "white" as a legal category, prohibiting interracial marriage. The color line is being drawn.

1705: The Virginia Slave Codes. The first comprehensive legal codification of racial hierarchy. Enslaved people are declared property. Violence against them is legalized. The law creates the category of "race" and assigns it consequences.

Late 18th century: The antislavery movement grows. Suddenly, the planter class needs a new justification. Religious arguments are failing. They need science — or something that looks like it.

Enter the "scientists."

The Junk Science of Racial Hierarchy

The 18th and 19th centuries saw an explosion of "scientific" attempts to prove what the slave economy needed to be true: that racial hierarchy was natural, inevitable, and ordained by biology.

Phrenology — the study of skull bumps to determine intelligence and character — was invented by Franz Joseph Gall in the early 1800s. Its practitioners claimed they could prove racial inferiority by feeling the shape of a person's head. The methodology was absurd: Gall based his theories on observations of family and friends, then retrofitted the "science" to match his prejudices. A 2017 Oxford University study using MRI technology confirmed what should have been obvious: there is no correlation between skull shape and personality traits.

Craniometry — measuring skull capacity to prove white superiority — was championed by Samuel Morton in the mid-1800s. Morton amassed a collection of over 1,000 human skulls and claimed his measurements proved a racial hierarchy. In 1981, paleontologist Stephen Jay Gould reexamined Morton's data in his book The Mismeasure of Man and exposed the fraud: Morton had cherry-picked his samples, ignored variables like nutrition and age, and manipulated his measurements to reach his predetermined conclusions.

Eugenics — the movement to "improve" the human race through selective breeding — took root in the early 20th century. In the United States, more than 60,000 people were forcibly sterilized between the 1920s and 1970s. The targets were disproportionately Black, Latino, Native American, and poor white — anyone deemed "unfit" by the ruling class.

And then eugenics went to Germany.

The Nazi regime, directly inspired by American eugenics programs, sterilized over 400,000 people and murdered more than 70,000 adults and 5,200 children in their "euthanasia" programs — before the Holocaust even began. Hitler himself praised American eugenics laws and kept a copy of Madison Grant's The Passing of the Great Race on his desk.

This is where "race science" leads. Always. Without exception.

What the Scientists Say Now

The scientific consensus is overwhelming. Here's what the experts have concluded:

The American Anthropological Association, in its 1998 Statement on Race: "The continued sharing of genetic materials has maintained all of humankind as a single species... present-day inequalities between so-called 'racial' groups are not consequences of their biological inheritance but products of historical and contemporary social, economic, educational, and political circumstances."

Geneticist Luigi Luca Cavalli-Sforza, who spent decades mapping human genetic variation: "The concept of race has failed to obtain any consensus."

Harvard geneticist David Reich, in 2018: "It is simply no longer possible to ignore average genetic differences among 'races'... But the traditional concept of race based on physical appearance is a biological misconception."

A 1985 survey of 1,200 scientists asked whether biological races exist in Homo sapiens. Only 16% of biologists agreed. Among cultural anthropologists, 53% explicitly rejected the concept. Those numbers have only grown more decisive in the decades since.

The science is settled. Race, as biology, does not exist.

Race, as a social and political invention? That's very real — and very much still with us.

What "Critical Race Theory" Actually Is

Which brings us to the current panic.

Critical Race Theory — CRT — is a legal framework developed in the 1970s and 80s by scholars like Derrick Bell, Kimberlé Crenshaw, and Richard Delgado. It examines how American laws and institutions have perpetuated racial inequality, even when those laws appear neutral on their face.

The classic example is redlining. In the 1930s, the federal government drew literal red lines around Black neighborhoods on maps and declared them too risky for mortgage lending. The policy didn't mention race explicitly — it talked about "neighborhood stability" and "investment risk." But the effect was to systematically deny Black Americans access to homeownership, the primary vehicle for building generational wealth in this country.

That's what CRT examines: how systems can produce racist outcomes even without racist intent. How the law can perpetuate inequality while appearing colorblind.

It's taught in law schools. Some graduate programs. That's it. It was never — at any point — part of K-12 curriculum in any state in America.

So why are we talking about it?

The Manufactured Panic

In March 2021, a conservative filmmaker named Christopher Rufo appeared on Fox News and said something remarkable. Not on camera — on Twitter, explaining his strategy:

"The goal is to have the public read something crazy in the newspaper and immediately think 'critical race theory.' We have successfully frozen their brand — 'critical race theory' — into the public conversation and are steadily driving up negative perceptions. We will eventually turn it toxic, as we put all of the various cultural insanities under that brand category."

He was explicit: the point wasn't accuracy. The point was to take a term most Americans had never heard of and turn it into a catch-all for anything about race that made white parents uncomfortable.

It worked.

Since 2021, more than 750 bills restricting the teaching of "CRT" or "divisive concepts" have been introduced in state legislatures. At least 18 states have passed such laws. The laws are deliberately vague, banning concepts like teaching that "the United States is fundamentally racist" or that any group bears "collective guilt" for historical wrongs.

Here in Tennessee, the law passed in 2021. It prohibits teaching 11 specific concepts, including that "an individual, by virtue of the individual's race or sex, bears responsibility for actions committed in the past by other members of the same race or sex."

Teachers report "chilling effects." They're afraid to teach about slavery. About the Civil Rights Movement. About the 1968 Memphis sanitation strike — which happened right here in Tennessee.

One Tennessee teacher put it this way: "I guess I was teaching critical race theory in my lesson about the sanitation strike."

The Irony

Here's what kills me about all of this.

The anti-CRT laws prove the point CRT makes.

Sociologist Victor Ray said it best: "Making laws outlawing CRT confirms the point that racism is embedded in the law."

Think about it. CRT argues that American laws and systems can perpetuate racial inequality even without explicit racist intent. And the response? Pass laws — vaguely written, selectively enforced, with disproportionate impact on how we teach the history of Black Americans — to silence that argument.

The laws don't mention race. They talk about "divisive concepts" and "collective guilt" and making students "feel discomfort." But the effect is to make teachers afraid to teach about slavery, Jim Crow, redlining, the Civil Rights Movement — anything that might make white students uncomfortable about American history.

That's exactly how CRT describes racist policy: neutral language, unequal impact.

It would be funny if it weren't so dangerous.

Why This Matters to Me

I grew up in a small town in East Tennessee. I didn't see a person of color until I moved to Knoxville at seventeen.

That wasn't an accident. That was design.

The same systems that drew redlines around Black neighborhoods in Memphis and Chicago drew different lines around places like Rocky Top. Not to keep us out — but to keep us separate. To keep poor whites and poor Black folks from recognizing what they had in common. To keep us fighting each other instead of fighting together.

The Coal Creek War happened in my backyard. In 1891, miners — Black and white — stood together against the convict leasing system that exploited them both. They burned the stockades. They freed the prisoners. They won.

That story wasn't in my textbooks.

And now there are people working very hard to make sure it never will be.

The Ugly Truth

Here's what I know:

Race isn't biology. It's a story — invented by people who needed to justify cruelty, maintained by people who benefit from division, and defended by people who would rather cling to comfortable lies than face uncomfortable truths.

There is one human species. One. We emerged from the same African ancestors. We carry 99.9% of the same genetic code. The differences we've been taught to see as fundamental — skin color, hair texture, facial features — are adaptations to different climates, as biologically significant as whether you can roll your tongue.

Nature didn't divide us. We did that to ourselves.

And we can undo it.

But not by banning books. Not by silencing teachers. Not by pretending history didn't happen.

Only by telling the truth.

— Heather C.
